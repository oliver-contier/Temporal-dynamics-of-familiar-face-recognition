Learning of unfamiliar faces (induced visual familiarity)

For instance, it is unclear how the representation of a previously unfamiliar face might change while becoming visually familiar, without acquiring any personal knowledge about that person (Gobbini & Haxby, 2006).
FFA:
This has been proposed to coincide with a more efficient analysis by the early visual areas of the "core system" (Gobbini & Haxby, 2006).
Amygdala:
The amygdala - an area associated with the evaluation of emotionally relevant stimuli (Breiter et al., 1996; Canli et al., 2002; Morris et al., 1996; Zalla et al., 2000) - has been shown to respond more strongly to unfamiliar than familiar faces (Gobbini et al., 2004; Leibenluft et al., 2004). This has been interpreted as a state of alertness when encountering an unfamiliar person (Gobbini & Haxby, 2007). However, this effect has been known to decrease upon repetition of the stimulus (Dubois et al., 1999, Kosaka et al., 2003; Schwarz et al., 2003).
Precuneus:
Furthermore, visually induced familiarity has been shown to evoke increased activity in the precuneus - an area associated with long-term memory retrieval (Burgess et al., 2001) and imagery (Fletcher et al., 1995; Ishai, Ungerleider, & Haxby, 2000). It has therefore been framed as a module involved in representing episodic memories linked to a familiar face (2007). We therefore expect activity of the precuneus in response to unfamiliar faces to increase during the course of the experiment, resulting in a diminished contrast between the response to familiar and unfamiliar faces in this region.
