Previous studies on habituation, adaptation, and learning:
So far, studies investigating such changes in the response to faces largely used paradigms like repetition suppression and adaptation (e.g. EEG repetition suppression in \citealp*{Caharel_2011}; fMRI adaptation in \citealp*{Eger_2005}; more references in these two papers). However, such methods zoom in on changes on a very short time scale and effects are generally interpreted more in terms of functional localisation but are not very informative to the question of how the neural representation of face familiarity changes over a longer and possible more naturalistic time scale. 
Two previous fMRI studies have focused on the changes in familiarity effects over a longer time scale (\citealp*{Gobbini_2006}; \citealp*{Kosaka_2003}).
\citet{Kosaka_2003} have investigated how the response to unfamiliar faces habituates over their experiment, which comprised of several functional runs. They found habituation effects in the right amygdala and left fusiform gyrus. The bilateral posterior cingulate gyrus however showed an increase of activity as the experiment proceeded. 
\citet{Gobbini_2006} on the other hand manipulated visual familiarity experimentally. Participants learned the visual appearance of initially unfamiliar faces in a behavioural task preceding an fMRI session. While visual familiarity led to a decrease of the response in the amygdala and fusiform gyrus, the precuneus showed an increased response.
It should be noted however that the anatomical location of this latter activation cluster also spanned parts of the posterior cingulate gyrus. 
A more recent study by \citet*{Natu_2015} has provided further evidence that face-sensitive areas in the fusiform gyrus contain useful patterns for classifying visually familiar versus unfamiliar faces. 
Together these results suggest that the posterior cingulate gyrus, precuneus, and amygdala are involved in the acquisition of visual familiarity in face perception (citation needed?). 
However, the possible habituation of the neural response to familiar faces remain unknown. 
The present study aims at investigating habituation of familiarity effects (i.e. the contrast Familiar  > Unfamiliar) while at the same time differentiating the contribution of changes in the response to familiar or unfamiliar faces respectively.

Learning of unfamiliar faces (induced visual familiarity)

For instance, it is unclear how the representation of a previously unfamiliar face might change while becoming visually familiar, without acquiring any personal knowledge about that person (Gobbini & Haxby, 2006).
FFA:
This has been proposed to coincide with a more efficient analysis by the early visual areas of the "core system" (Gobbini & Haxby, 2006).
Amygdala:
The amygdala - an area associated with the evaluation of emotionally relevant stimuli (Breiter et al., 1996; Canli et al., 2002; Morris et al., 1996; Zalla et al., 2000) - has been shown to respond more strongly to unfamiliar than familiar faces (Gobbini et al., 2004; Leibenluft et al., 2004). This has been interpreted as a state of alertness when encountering an unfamiliar person (Gobbini & Haxby, 2007). However, this effect has been known to decrease upon repetition of the stimulus (Dubois et al., 1999, Kosaka et al., 2003; Schwarz et al., 2003).
Precuneus:
Furthermore, visually induced familiarity has been shown to evoke increased activity in the precuneus - an area associated with long-term memory retrieval (Burgess et al., 2001) and imagery (Fletcher et al., 1995; Ishai, Ungerleider, & Haxby, 2000). It has therefore been framed as a module involved in representing episodic memories linked to a familiar face (2007). We therefore expect activity of the precuneus in response to unfamiliar faces to increase during the course of the experiment, resulting in a diminished contrast between the response to familiar and unfamiliar faces in this region.
